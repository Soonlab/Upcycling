**Subject: Submission to *mSystems* — "Comparative genomics of 111 livestock-waste MAGs nominates novel *Sphingobacterium* species with a vertically inherited *ure–cah* module for alkali-tolerant MICP"**

Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript for consideration as a Research Article in *mSystems*. The work is a systems-level comparative-genomic analysis of **111 metagenome-assembled genomes** (MAGs) recovered from four livestock-waste microbiomes, and its methodology, scope, and open-science deliverables are, in our view, a close fit for *mSystems*' tradition of publishing genome-resolved, ecologically contextualised microbial-systems analyses.

**What the paper does.** We integrate eight orthogonal analyses — Bakta annotation, Panaroo pan-genomics, GTDB-Tk/IQ-TREE phylogenomics, skani whole-genome ANI, reciprocal-best mmseqs2 AAI, DRAM metabolic reconstruction, dbCAN-HMMER CAZyme profiling, and permutation-tested trait-module enrichment — into a single reproducible pipeline (https://github.com/Soonlab/Upcycling). Applied to the livestock-waste MAG panel, the pipeline identifies a six-member set of **functionally convergent** MAGs (four *Sphingobacterium* + two *Pseudomonas*\_E) that retain a complete *ureABCDEFG–cah* module characteristic of microbially induced carbonate precipitation (MICP). Within each lineage, the module shows no mobile-element flanking and no regional GC deviation, consistent with vertical inheritance rather than horizontal acquisition. Ten-thousand-iteration permutation with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR identifies nine trait modules significantly enriched (q < 0.05) in the hero lineages — including Mrp Na⁺/H⁺ antiporters (fold change 10.9), oxidative-defence enzymes (4.8), glycoside hydrolases (4.7) and carbohydrate-binding modules (9.8) — forming a mechanistically coherent stack for survival and catalysis in alkaline, polysaccharide-rich slurry. Principal coordinates analysis reveals a striking dichotomy: pan-genome structure is primarily explained by GTDB genus (pseudo-F = 8.21, p = 0.001), whereas trait-module structure is primarily explained by waste source (pseudo-F = 34.19, p = 0.001). Finally, pairwise ANI against 63 RefSeq *Sphingobacterium* genomes identifies **S13 and S16 as candidate novel species** (< 95 % ANI), nominating them as priority chassis for future experimental validation of waste-coupled MICP.

**Why *mSystems*.** The manuscript embodies the five features that *mSystems* has historically prioritised: (i) **systems scope** (111 MAGs × 98 metabolic modules × 38 trait subcategories); (ii) **rigorous statistical controls** for effect-size claims (permutation with FDR and bootstrap CIs, instead of unqualified fold changes); (iii) **open-science deliverables** (all code, derived tables, intermediate files and figures are already available on GitHub under CC-BY / MIT); (iv) **translation-ready framing** (the analysis directly supports circular-bioeconomy MICP applications in livestock waste management); (v) **honest treatment of limitations** — we explicitly flag that the study is computational and identify the specific wet-lab experiments required to complete the biotechnological argument.

**Scope disclosure.** This is an in-silico study. We have not performed urease-activity assays, CaCO₃-yield measurements or field tests; these are clearly framed as the next experimental step. We believe, and hope the editorial team agrees, that a statistically tested and fully reproducible comparative-genomic resource paper is valuable to the field in its own right, and that requiring wet-lab validation as a precondition would unnecessarily delay dissemination of a useful target list to the MICP community.
